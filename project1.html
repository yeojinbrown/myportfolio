SMART HOME - VOICE UX

Samsung SmartThings, the latest Internet of Things (IoT) home automation service by Samsung allows home devices such as lights, thermostats, outlets, sensors and more to be controlled through a smartphone application and a unique Hub device.

Goal: Allow users to interact with smart home Hub via Voice.

My Role: Lead designer of the 'Voice User Experience' for this Smart Home Hub.

DESIGN PRINCIPLES:

Understand user intent by understanding ambiguous voice commands.

Allow for natural conversational style.

Induce delight in conversations.

PROCESS:

The project began with understanding of the kinds of home automation devices and sensors that Smartthings provides. Secondary research included reading articles about smart home device usage, IoT videos to understand perception of home automation. Also all Smartthings compatible products listed on their website (screenshot below) were analyzed as to home environment usage.

The sheer volume of devices that Smartthings supports was overwhelming. So essentially for the scenario of a fully functional smart home, Voice dialogues had to be written for controlling doors, windows, garage doors, temperatures, lights, lamps, outlets or for getting information on basement, kitchen sink moisture readings, pet presence, baby crib safety and many others.

When as humans we naturally tend to say "It's too bright in here" as opposed to "Dim the ambient lights in the living room" it becomes a challenge for VUI design as now we had to figure out algorithms to understand which room user might be in.

EXPERIENCE ROOM BODYSTORMING:

Sitting in Bangalore, designing VUIs for the North American user group was hugely helped by an Experience room that was set up at Samsung. We worked in collaboration with developers to set up the devices and prototype automation or control scenarios. This fueled insights on what the end-user experience would be like.
Photographs are NDA restricted.

KEY DELIVERABLES:

User-home dialogue flows, Voice UI dialogue principles and UX proposals for home Voice UX.

User-Home dialogues were captured in flow charts for exhaustive set of use-cases.

DEMO at CES 2016:

The Home Hub with Voice control was prototyped in partnership with Seoul HQ Design team.

Demo was given at CES 2016 by President Dr. W. P. Hong, as part of Samsung's vision of a unified IoT platform.

WHAT I LEARNT

Designing for the plethora of IoT devices, while being empathetic to the ambiguity with which users recall device names was challenging.
I gained deep understanding of home automation technology and technical implementation. Beyond the commonly practiced UI and GUI aspects of Design, I learnt and developed a stronghold of Voice UI design.

CROSS-PLATFORM VOICE EXPERIENCE

S-Voice is Samsung's personal assistant on all flagship smartphones and wearables that lets user access major phone functionalities - making calls, playing music, setting alarms or seeking weather information and more.

Goal: Offer a unified cross-platform Voice experience that enables users to access major phone functionalities.

My Role: Voice UX Design - UX principles, Dialogue management & UI design.

Key contribution: As part of the Next Experiences with Technology (NXT) team at Samsung R&D Bangalore, I designed and enhanced Voice UX for flagship phones Galaxy S5 (for Korean, Chinese markets), Galaxy S6 (Korean, Chinese and US markets) and wearables like earpiece device - Samsung Gear Circle and smartwatch - Samsung Gear S, S2.

  Check my LinkedIn recommendation.

  An article inspired by this work.

DESIGN OUTCOME:

Voice breaks the hierarchy of touch UI. When a user's tasks are fairly straightforward, Voice can offer quicker task completion. Here are the scenarios we designed Voice UI for.

Process & Methods:

PROJECT REQUIREMENT DOCUMENT CREATION:

With the goal to apply a layer of Voice interaction onto existing smartphone experience, first a detailed study of the phone app/functionality on which this layer is to be applied would be done. Example, for 'Navigation', all possible scenarios where a user may use Voice for navigating to various locations would be brainstormed.

Brainstorming sessions using visual cues were done to elicit scenarios for what users may ask a Voice agent while using Navigation. This exhaustive set of scenarios would be captured in the PRD document.

Designed to voice enable 22+ apps/ functionalities such as Call, SMS, Music, Alarms, Schedule - events and reminders, Weather and third party services as Wolfram Alpha and Yelp.

DEFINING DIALOGUE PRINCIPLES:

Dialogue principles acted as Design guidelines for S-Voice's responses to user queries/utterances.

  1. NATURAL SPOKEN UTTERANCES - Voice being a rather natural mode of interaction, Voice assistant must understand when user is natural or ambiguous in their spoken utterances. Example, it is more natural to articulate an intent to create alarm by saying "Wake me up at 6 AM" (creates alarm for 6 AM) or "Don't disturb me till 7 AM" (puts phone to silent profile and sets an alarm for 7 AM).

  2. DISAMBIGUATION - Wherever needed, employ clarification or disambiguation flows. Example, if user says "Call John", take decision and make a call to John Doe (most frequently called contact), but until that frequency can be guessed, revert asking "There are two contacts by that name John Smith and John Doe. Which one do you want?". Aim to balance shorter dialogue flows and accurate intent capture.

  3. ERROR RECOVERY - An important principle for dialogue design was to allow for efficient error recovery. For instance, if user said "Text John I'll be late", system responds "Alright, your message to John Doe says I'll be late. Ready to send?", we must understand if user says "No I meant that for Joanne Smith".

  4. SEAMLESS TOPIC SWITCHING - Allow for seamless conversations. So user can ask for "What's the time difference between Paris and here?" and follow-up saying "How's the weather there next week?" Keeping memory of conversational context is key.

Tools: Post-its, lots of talking to people - guerilla techniques to get insights on expectations from Voice assistant, role-playing of user-agent with team members, secondary research on Siri, Cortana, Google assistant.

Dialogue principles not only guided the UX of dialogue flows, but also served as guideline to technical development teams and language experts.

DETAILED DIALOGUE MANAGEMENT:

To capture detailed S-Voice-user conversation flows, Voice User Interface - VUI guides were created on Microsoft Visio. These capture the key user utterances, intent behind utterances, S-Voice responses and follow-up user requests/responses.

Detailed VUI flows capture user's conversations with smartphone, earpiece device, smartwatch and car. These dialogue flow documents and UI design specs were shared with technical implementation teams.

Working in close collaboration with developers, user as well as technology empathic use-cases were captured in the VUIs.

CROSS-PLATFORM EXPERIENCE DESIGN:

Beyond the smartphone platform, Voice UX was also designed for car, smartwatch and completely eyes-free interaction over the earpiece device. Bodystorming techniques and informal guerilla research with users of smartwatches, bluetooth headsets, etc. gave insights about actual physical contexts of use. These led to design of platform-specific VUIs.

ROLE-PLAYING:

The dialogues designed would then be role-played by team members to 'hear' the experience. This was supplemented with paper prototypes to show on-screen supporting UI for S-Voice. This helped greatly in lieu of conducting usability tests with VUIs due to time and technical constraints.

INCREMENTAL INNOVATION PROPOSALS:

So that users can have a natural conversational experience with S-Voice, concepts were created to aim for shorter dialogue flows, better error handling, remembering conversational context, etc. UX proposals were designed and navigated across various technical teams at SRI-B and Korean headquarters to improve S-Voice UX.

Details cannot be disclosed as per NDA.

WHAT I LEARNT

Empathy to the natural-ness of Voice and users' relative unfamiliarity to Voice as a medium of interaction enabled me to design more human-like rather than machine-like interactions.

I developed and percolated a new Voice UI design skill in the UX team too!

I gathered knowledge of deep technical implementation that surrounds this emerging technology space.

I can confidently say ASR, NLU, TTS and NLG aren't just alphabet soup for me!

CONTEXTUAL INTELLIGENCE UX

If you happen to ping an old friend after a long time or plan meetings with colleagues or make dinner plans over texts, how can your phone enhance your messaging app experience?

Goal: Enhance messaging experience of users by leveraging intelligence of a context-aware engine.

My Role: UX researcher and designer.

Duration: 3 months, Sep - Nov '16.
Done as part of 'UX for Intelligence' study with Prof. Dr. Jason Hong at HCII, CMU. Detailed documentation of this project.

DESIGN OUTCOME:

Based on user's conversations in messaging apps, helpful suggestions or information is given to them contextually.
Here, user and her friend are planning to meet and the contextual engine brings up weather information indicating possibility of showers around that time.

More suggestion scenarios:

PROCESS:

Longitudinal data collection: A two week longitudinal study was conducted with 17 participants to understand current messaging app behavior i.e. what are they using messaging apps for?

Messaging data collected was analyzed and themes for user needs such as updating status - what they’re up to, co-ordinating meet-ups, sharing location or phone content, etc. were identified.

Brainstorming: Could we matchmake the abilities of context mining engine to user needs and enhance the experience?

Ideas and concepts generated were clustered into themes of user needs, example in-chat content exchange, icebreakers, etc. through whiteboard discussions and paper prototypes.

Short-term and long-term concepts: Concepts were then segregated based on what was achievable with existing contextual intelligence of the engine. Here are some conceptual illustrations:

SPEED DATING:

For validating concepts, it was important to know their perceived utility from the users. Speed dating scenario-based paper prototypes with 5 participants helped understand how much intelligence is too much intelligence!

DESIGN ITERATIONS:

For the concepts, that received positive feedback in terms of utility, privacy and non-spookiness of intelligence, those that were technically feasible were chosen. Next step was to design in-time contextual suggestion/information in the messaging app experience.

After several stages of iteration, a simple design for showing contextual suggestion was chosen. In the final iteration, intelligent suggestion scrolls in after the latest message as soon as an opportunity to make a suggestion is identified.

With an informative and actionable tone, the text indicates what to expect and user may decide to see details by tapping on the text.

Some of the scenarios like breaking ice with an old friend, bringing up phone content in messaging and others are being implemented for Whatsapp and Facebook Messenger apps by the development team.

WHAT I LEARNT

My most significant learning was to resolve the ambiguity of a new technology, an intelligence solution, that is unadopted but has potential to make contextually useful suggestions.

This project being research-based, had a lot of scope for exploration. The ideas discussed here may be challenging to implement, example in the image above a reminder called "Take book" is created which may be difficult sentence to construct, but an image capture of the latest conversation may also help if intent is correctly captured.

What I tried to experiment here is if people will be willing to forgive non-useful or irrelevant suggestions in exchange for moments of value when the suggestions are in fact relevant... provided, the interaction is designed to be non-intrusive and with a suggestive tone.

CARMA - APP FOR TWEENS & PARENTS

Tweens these days are engaged in various social and interpersonal development activities such as theatre, football, music or hanging out at cafes with friends. They depend on parents for transportation. But more often than not prior engagements prevent parents from providing transport for their tween's last minute plans.

Goal: Design a solution that enables tween transportation and better tween-parent co-ordination.

My Role: UX researcher and designer.

Duration: 1 month, Nov '16 (as part of IxD studio project)

DESIGN OUTCOME:

A system that makes parent tween travel co-ordination easier. Busy parents don't have to travel around their tween kids at the last minute. They off-load this transportation task to a trustworthy pool of drivers. But it's not so easy for tweens. They must do good deeds for these drivers before asking them for rides. It's good Karma!

No wait, it's Carma!

PROCESS:

Outlining the Ecosystem: Our first step was to explore our current knowledge about tween and parent coordination of social activities.

Guerilla Research: We interviewed 5 tweens and 6 tween parents, to understand how tween events are co-ordinated and if and what are some associated pain-points. Some parents also shared artifacts like calendar or bulletin boards that they use to co-ordinate activities of their kids.

Key insights: Parents usually grant permission to tween activities, but this doesn't translate to transporting them around. They don't like it when tweens assume they are free. And parents want tweens to be independent in making their plans.

Personas: Building on our insights from research, we developed the two primary personas - Tween persona and Parent persona.

Brainstorming and Scenario building: Utilizing reframing techniques we challenged ourselves to see beyond immediately obvious problem resolution ideas and move towards re-thinking current ecosystem and pain-points, we generated a wide range of ideas.

Ideas were consolidated into 3 major concepts and labelled - 'C' for Concept and 'I' for Interaction, since some of the ideas were not complete concepts but more like interface interactions.

SYSTEM DESIGN:

Speed dating: The concept storyboards were discussed with 2 parents for usefulness. The idea 'Pool of pre-approved drivers' was most appreciated. What is this pool? - Parents often rely on their friends, neighborhood friends, older siblings or other parents to help out if they are busy.

Why would the pool drivers help out? - Tweens are required to do good deeds for these drivers by helping them out in small ways and entering their karma story into the app interface. Parents see this on their version of the app and allow tweens to ride with drivers.

TWEEN & PARENT APPS DESIGN:

Bearing in mind the features that both parent and tween apps would have to facilitate, we quickly laid out dirty mock-ups to outline the navigation of the app.

Guerilla testing with Medium-fi prototypes: The screens were quickly mocked up in Sketch into various iterations, printed and peer feedback was elicited to get insights on what the screens conveyed and flow of key sub-tasks - creating a ride request and adding a Karma story.

M-HEALTH SYSTEM DESIGN

People use mobile health (m-health) applications for assistance in maintaining good health practices, specially in case of lifestyle diseases such as diabetes, high BP or for quitting smoking. While these apps generate some curiosity, in the long run people discontinue use with high drop-out rates.

Outcome: I pursued this HCI research problem and designed a system wherein 'patient motivation is ensured through active doctor participation in the journey of treatment'. As a use-case, a tablet app was designed to impart doctor-controlled therapy for the problem of weak eyesight.
A research paper was published. You can have a look.

My Process: Secondary research, Object-oriented system design, Weak eye-sight case study, Tablet app design, Prototyping, Usability Testing.

Duration: 8 months, Aug '12 - Mar '13 (Undergraduate thesis)

SECONDARY RESEARCH:

Key insight: In the traditional doctor consultation model, "partnership" between patient and doctor is a strong motivator.
Observation: Literature-based heuristic analysis of existing m-health applications showed that 'doctor consultation' is absent from m-healthcare.

OBJECT ORIENTED SYSTEM DESIGN:

Various 'objects' i.e. stakeholders, users, technological requirements, etc. involved in system design were identified through brainstorming and secondary research. A number of system use-cases were modelled into Unified Modelling Language (UML) diagrams.

SYSTEM DESIGN AND FEATURES:

Doctor “prescribes" a suitable m-health application and participates through this app in the patient's journey. He keeps track of patient health and modifies treatment when necessary.

THERAPY USECASE - WEAK EYESIGHT:

'Therapeutic' mode of treatment offers adequate patient self-regulation and opportunities for doctor supervision. Target health problem: Weak eyesight, which results from aging or work-related stress was chosen.

TABLET APP DESIGN:

For offering therapeutic eyesight treatment, Bates Method of alternative vision therapy was implemented. In this method eye exercises, relaxation techniques and proper diet are prescribed to ensure improvement of eyesight. Various such features were identified.
'Therapy-related tasks and features were written down in the form of labels such as “Diary entry”, “Notifications”, “Exercise”, "Send Report to doc", etc. Card sorting technique was employed to outline information architecture.

TRICKLE - AN INSTALLATION CONCEPT

Public Wi-Fi networks at airports, restaurants and cafes is an amazing perk, but it comes at a risk. It is very easy for anyone to access data about your online activity if you are connected to a public Wi-Fi.

We wanted to inform the public about this and chose to tell this story through a light and sound installation.

My Role: Concept creation, Interaction design, Video.

Duration: 4 weeks, Aug '16 (Team project for course - Reactive Spaces and Media Architecture)

CONCEPT:

When someone connects to our (evil-twin) public Wi-Fi network and their online activity is tracked, our installation shows a trickling down pattern using lights.

This pattern is user-specific and tries to follow movement of the person for whom it identifies data leakage.

The installation uses different colors of lights that relate to different apps and services that are identified in person's online activity.

Also, when an incoming notification on any of those apps is received the notification sound of that app is played by the installation - to re-inforce threat to personal data.

MAKING:

Choosing location: We wanted to position this installation in a public Wi-Fi environment so we chose the Starbucks on Forbes and Craig Street.

Re-iterating our goal: We want to inform people about being careful what they browse on public Wi-Fis, not stop using them.
So we decided to set up the installation just outside Starbucks and not inside (permission for inside would be much more difficult too :) so people can be reminded about it as they would enter the public Wi-Fi area.

Technology: The installation was fabricated into a 8' by 6' cardboard with lights programmed into a trickling animation. We then set up an evil-twin public Wi-Fi network called 'Starbucks 2'. Using Address Resolution Protocol spoofing technique one of our team mates was able to intercept data packets being accessed by people connected to this network. When they stepped out of Starbucks, the trickling lights would unobtrusively communicate some kind of data leakage.

CARNATIC MUSIC TUTOR

Carnatic music is a classical Indian music style from southern part of India. Instruction of this style is mostly prevalent in south India, making it hard to find teachers in other parts of the country. Also, unlike other forms of music, there were no computer-based tutors available.

Outcome: We designed a desktop tutor proof of concept application for learning basics of Carnatic music.
A research paper was published. You can have a look.

Process: Literature study, Questionnaire Surveys, Interviews, Participatory exercise with music experts, Instruction design, Information architecture, Prototyping, SUS based user testing.

Duration: 3 months, Jan '12 - Mar '12 (Team of 2, undergraduate design studio)

DATA COLLECTION:

To understand stages of music learning and difficulties encountered, questionnaire survey was conducted with 20 students. 6 teachers were interviewed for the techniques they employed to instruct lessons, conduct practice and test student skills. For sensitizing to Carnatic music, 12 experts with over 10 years of experience were interviewed.

PARTICIPATORY DATA SYNTHESIS:

All the data gathered from interviews and surveys was synthesized in two stages. In the first stage, we began to unearth characteristics of learning relevant to various stages of music instruction and corresponding tutor features. In the second stage, we collaborated with 3 experts of Carnatic music to rate these features in an order of importance pertaining to the beginner learning stage.

LESSON INSTRUCTION DESIGN:

Research showed that ear training was absolutely pertinent to Carnatic music instruction. Therefore, every lesson had an ear training module at the beginning. Among other findings include - Changes in pitch is more sensitive in Carnatic music than Western classical and Hindustani syles of music so a special module called 'Identify notes' was added for deliberate training of notes relevant to a particular lesson, as guided by music experts.

Other features include showing progress bar to students, regular revision of previous lessons before starting daily lesson, mandatory testing and practice before beginning a new lesson and so on.

TUTOR INTERFACE DESIGN:

Multiple iterations were designed via rough dirty paper prototyping.

WIREFRAMING & PROTOTYPING:

Screens were mocked up in Illustrator and high-fidelity screens were mocked up in Photoshop and used for building a working proof of concept prototype in Adobe Flash.

USABILITY TESTING:

Usability test of the tutor was conducted with 5 participants. They were asked to take a lesson, perform practice over a music keyboard, give a test and self reflect on the analysis that tutor shows. On a System Usability Scale, the tutor received a score of 73 out of 100.
